Travel

midnight special

let it shine its ever lasting light on you

July 01, 2006 Edition 1

Graham Howe

W e've been waiting for this moment for days. Crossing into the Arctic Circle, we enter the legendary land of the midnight sun. A spontaneous cheer goes up from the ship's company. We are journeying into an imaginary country of Norse lore of giants and trolls, witches and water sprites, mermaids and serpents, Vikings and knights.

From the deck, we spot an armillary sphere of the globe which marks the polar sirkelen on a rocky island called Vikingen. An invisible line circles the globe at 66,5° latitude, marking the southernmost point at which the midnight sun shines 24 hours a day in mid-summer. In a zone with the navigational exactitude of seafarers, I feel the need to be precise, noting in my journal that it is 07:09:25 on June 9 2006.

It has been light all night since we sailed north from Bergen on the MS Kong Harald. We sleep by shutting the curtains tightly and burying our heads in warm pillows. At first, it is disorienting - though preferable to the dark Polar days of the mid-winter months. This is a land of extremes - where the natural elements like the light play tricks. If you visit in winter, the northern lights put on a magical show called the aurora borealis.

We pass the threshold into the Arctic Circle. To the west lies Hestmann Island - named after a knight turned to stone after his intense love for a woman made him forget the sunrise. They say he shot his last arrow through a troll's hat, the shape of a mountain peak which rises out of the sea. To the east lies Svartisen glacier, the second largest in Norway, beneath icy peaks which soar into the Scandinavian sky.

Leading the celebrations, King Neptune in fancy dress welcomes the newcomers to his realm, baptising passengers with a cup of icy water down the back of their shirts, a warming glass of sherry and an Arctic certificate. Anita Heiberg, a crew member, says, "The Arctic Circle divides Norway into north and south with different cultures, customs and capitals." Shrugging off a sudden rain squall, she adds, "In Norway there is no such thing as bad weather - only bad clothes for the weather".

Halvor Vinje, captain of the MS Kong Harald, looks like a silver-whiskered Norse mariner of old. Stroking his handlebar moustache like a Cheshire cat, the man with the braided cap and brass-buttoned uniform has a delightful sense of humour. In a quaint speech, he says, "I am the Captain, ruler of this ship - capricious and bad-tempered. To enjoy your adventure you have to treat well your wife, husband, family, fellow travellers, crew, whales, mermaids, birds, fish and crabs."

We certainly eat well on the catch of fish in the smart dining-room. Norway, a nation with a rich fishing heritage, is a pescatoric paradise. A sumptuous daily lunch buffet showcases the fruits of the sea - salmon gravlax, smoked eel, prawns, crab, mussels, lobster, mackerel, herring, halibut, salted cod and cod roe. We could, however, do without the bloody minke whale carpaccio euphemistically called Vestfjorden ham - a traditional whalers' dish sent back to the kitchen by many protesting passengers

According to mariner's legend, a "first tripper" - a sailor on his first voyage - has to doff his hat to Vagekallen, a mountain peak named after an old Viking king. We observe the ritual when passing one of the ancient fishing villages on the archipelago of islands beneath mighty Lofotveggen, a 100 kilometre-long wall of snow, ice and granite. The simple wooden cabins of Lofot are a sanctuary for the 3 000 fishermen who share the wilderness with 450 000 puffins - and all the tourists in summer.

The very landscape is the stuff of legend. On Midsummer's Eve - the solstice when the midnight sun is at its highest - witches are said to dance on Hornelen, a jagged mountain ascended by the great Viking king Olav. Every island, rock, fjord and natural landmark tells a tale - evocatively named after chiefs, princesses, an epic sea battle or a fairytale like the waterfalls of the Seven Sisters (who dance naked down the mountainside) and the Courtier who flirts playfully with them across the fjord.

The fjords are fabulous. You can see why National Geographic Traveller rated Norway Top World Destination in 2004. Melting snow on the jagged peaks creates myriad silvery waterfalls cascading from the mountains down to the fjords. Sailing through Geiranger fjord, the pearl of the west country fjords, the massive rock walls carved by ice-age glaciers seem close enough to touch from the top deck. Picturesque villages and towns lie scattered like jewels in the emerald green valleys on the shore.

The closest we get to a troll is in Troll Fjord. Every passenger is out on deck at midnight. In the narrowest passage of the cruise, the ship squeezes between the sheer sides of the 100m wide fjord. Waterfalls tumbling down the granite walls splash us as we pass beneath. The trolls are close enough to throw stones at the ship, but we're reliably informed they have been asleep for 1 000 years since waking up to the Battle of Troll Fjord in 1880 when fishermen fought over a huge catch trapped in the inlet.

We join a shore excursion, heading south by coach to the Salt Fjord. Bodo is renowned for its Saltstraumen current, one of the strongest tides in the world where vast quantities of water are forced through the narrow straits at a speed of 29km an hour.

The resultant maelstrom - the Norwegian word for these powerful whirlpools - has been mythologised in stories by Edgar Allen Poe and Jules Verne. Sea birds and fishermen line the banks, catching the silver flash of fish rising in the rapid current.

The MS Kong Harald finds a passage along a spectacular coastline through a maze of islands, skerries (reefs) and fjords. She is one of a dozen vessels on Norway's famous Hurtigruten route - the coastal express which, apart from wartime, has operated all-year round every day since the late 19th century.

The exhilarating trip between Bergen and Kirkenes on the Russian border takes 6-11 days on a 24 000 kilometre voyage, calling at 34 villages and towns along the way. The scenery on what is often called "the world's most beautiful voyage" is simply breathtaking

The Hurtigruten fleet is an unusual combination of working cargo, ferry and passenger boats. A lifeline for the remote communities in the north of Norway, the boats deliver fresh produce and goods to isolated villages which are often landlocked by snow and ice during the long, cold winters. While the crew offload cars and cargo at the quayside, passengers go ashore to visit medieval towns, museums and markets. Guided shore excursions include husky sledding, bird safaris, glacier and mountain trips, and a tour by snow mobile to a Sami community in Lappland in the far north.

Tromso, the gateway to the Arctic, is one of the highlights of the voyage. Near the main channel into the port we pass the site of the Tirpitz, the German warship sunk by British bombers in November 1944. The frontier town known as "the Paris of the North" was a trading-post for whalers, sealers and trappers for centuries. Many famous Arctic explorers set off from here like Nansen, Amundsen and Andree.

We come across a statue of Roald Amundsen in the heart of Old Tromso. Exhibits at the Polar Museum cover the fascinating history of Arctic expeditions as well as barbaric whaling traditions.

The grim harpoons are a tragic reminder that Norway (along with Japan) still hunts whales in contravention of the moratorium by the International Whaling Commission. An old sealing ship in a glass house on the quayside is another sad legacy of the cruel hunts which continue today in the Arctic Circle. The bearded seals and bizarre fish in the aquarium at Polaria, a high-tech Arctic adventure centre on the waterfront are fortunately alive and well.

At midnight, midday, dawn and dusk, the forklifts are always at work moving containers on a quayside somewhere on the rugged coast of Norway. At 5 o'clock one morning, we wake up to Hammerfest, the world's most northerly town. Napoleon, the Swedes, the Russians and the Germans fought for control of this strategic fishing port which lies at roughly the same latitude as northernmost Greenland, Labrador and Siberia.

Strolling around the deserted streets, we come across fibreglass statues of the polar bears which roam the area during winter. We are hoping to join the Royal and Ancient Polar Bear Society - which has already enrolled 200 000 members - but the exhibition of stuffed birds and bears from the town's trapper past is closed at this early hour. Instead we buy t-shirts bearing the legend

Norway Land of the Midnight Sun.

The closest we come to a polar bear are the stuffed ones at a souvenir shop on the waterfront at Honningsvag - and at a pub where I quaff a glass of ice-cold Polar lager at the northernmost micro-brewery in the world.

The North Cape Festival is well underway when we disembark from the MS Kong Harald. Brass bands and village choirs compete enthusiastically on the post office, shop and bank steps, surprising us visitors with songs like Monty Python's Always look on the bright side of Life.

The Arctic road ends at the North Cape, the northernmost point in Europe. The headland rises 307 metres above the sea, 2 080 kilometres from the North Pole.

The setting is as sensational as the landmark itself. The North Cape was named in 1553 by Richard Chancellor, a British seafarer looking for the north-east passage to India. A century later, an Italian explorer wrote, "I am standing at the edge of the world. This is where the world and my curiosity ends and I can return home satisfied."

We echo his sentiments, heading for the end of our cruise at Kirkenes on the Russian border.
