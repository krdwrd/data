</kw></h2>

&nbsp;<br>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">
Chronic increased pulmonary blood flow is considered a pre-requisite</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">for the induction of advanced vascular lesions in pulmonary</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">arterial hypertension in congenital heart defects. The aim of</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">the present study was to characterise the effects of increased</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">pulmonary flow induced by an aortocaval shunt in the monocrotaline</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">rat model for pulmonary hypertension in terms of survival, haemodynamics,</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">pathology and histology.</kw><sup> </sup></strong></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Male Wistar rats were injected with monocrotaline followed by</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">the creation of an abdominal aortocaval shunt. Animals were</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">sacrificed when displaying symptoms of weight loss or dyspnoea,</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">4–5 weeks after the creation of the shunt.</kw><sup> </sup></strong></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Echocardiography identified increased ventricular dimensions</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">in shunted rats and right ventricular hypertrophy in monocrotaline-treated</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">rats. At similar pulmonary artery pressures, shunted monocrotaline</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">rats displayed higher morbidity and mortality, increased pulmonary-to-systemic</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">artery pressure ratios and increased right ventricular hypertrophy</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">compared with nonshunted monocrotaline rats. Histological assessment</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">demonstrated increased number and diameter of pre-acinar pulmonary</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">arteries. Intra-acinar vessel remodelling and occlusion occurred</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">to a similar extent in shunted and nonshunted monocrotaline</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">rats.</kw><sup> </sup></strong></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">In conclusion, increased pulmonary blood flow in monocrotaline-induced</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">pulmonary hypertension is associated with increased morbidity,</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">mortality, and unfavourable haemodynamic and cardiac effects.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">These effects could be attributed to more pronounced right heart</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">failure rather than to altered intra-acinar pulmonary vessel</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">remodelling.</kw><sup> </sup></strong></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">



Patients with congenital heart disease associated with systemic-to-pulmonary</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">shunts, increasing both pulmonary blood flow and pressure, develop</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">characteristic pulmonary vascular lesions as concentric-laminar</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">intimal fibrosis and plexiform lesions. These vascular lesions</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">are characterised by vascular smooth muscle and endothelial</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">cell proliferation. In contrast, in patients with congenital</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">heart defects and isolated increased pulmonary artery pressure</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">(</kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">P</kw></i><sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">AP</kw></sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">), these lesions almost never develop </kw><a href="#R1"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">1</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R2"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">2</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. The current</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">authors hypothesise that, in congenital heart defects, increased</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">pulmonary blood flow, in addition to increased pressure, is</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">a pre-requisite for the development of the hallmark lesions</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of advanced pulmonary arterial hypertension.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Animal models with an isolated increase in </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">P</kw></i><sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">AP</kw></sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">e.g.</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> induced</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">by the toxic alkaloid monocrotaline, show pulmonary vascular</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">remodelling, but fail to display the more advanced lesions as</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">described previously </kw><a href="#R3"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">3</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">–</kw><a href="#R5"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">5</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

The role of increased pulmonary blood flow has been explored</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">in animal models </kw><a href="#R6"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">6</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. In rat models with isolated increased flow</kw><sup> </sup><a href="#R7"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">7</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R8"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">8</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, a moderate increase in pulmonary pressure and medial hypertrophy</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">has been observed, but only after a prolonged period of exposure.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

When the additional effect of flow was explored in existing</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">rat models for pulmonary arterial hypertension, cellular occlusion</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of small vessels, referred to as neo-intimal formation, was</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">only observed in animals with increased pulmonary blood flow</kw><sup> </sup><a href="#R9"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">9</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">–</kw><a href="#R11"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">11</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. In contrast, one recent report suggests a favourable</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">effect of increased blood flow. In this study, rats with an</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">abdominal aortocaval shunt in addition to monocrotaline, showed</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">a reduction in neo-intimal formation </kw><a href="#R10"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">10</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

In the present study, the role of increased pulmonary flow induced</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">by an abdominal aortocaval shunt in the monocrotaline rat model</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">is explored in further detail </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">via</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> haemodynamical, echocardiographical</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">and histopathological characterisation, with special emphasis</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">on neo-intimal formation.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Experimental protocol</kw></strong><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

In total, 39 male Wistar rats (250–350&nbsp;g; fed </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">ad</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">libitum</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">) were housed under diurnal lighting conditions. Animal</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">care and experiments were conducted according to the Dutch Animal</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Experimental Act. The Erasmus University Animal Care and Use</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Committee (Rotterdam, The Netherlands) approved the experimental</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">protocol.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Rats were randomly assigned to one of four experimental groups:</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">1) control (CON: n = 11); 2) increased pulmonary flow by means</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of an abdominal aortocaval shunt (AV: n = 8); 3) increased </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">P</kw></i><sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">AP</kw></sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">,</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">induced by monocrotaline (MC: n = 8); and 4) the combination</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of both interventions (MC+AV: n = 12). Monocrotaline (Sigma</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Chemical Co, St. Louis, MO, USA; 60&nbsp;mg·kg</kw><sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">–1</kw></sup><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">subcutaneously) or vehicle were injected. Sham or AV-shunt surgery</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">was performed 7 days later as described by Garcia and Diebold</kw><sup> </sup><a href="#R12"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">12</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> under pentobarbital anaesthesia (60&nbsp;mg·kg</kw><sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">–1</kw></sup><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">intraperitoneally). Shunt patency was verified visually as swelling,</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">colour change and pulsation of the caval vein.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Animals were weighed, watched for dyspnoea and sacrificed when</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">a 15% weight loss or debilitating dyspnoea occurred. Matched</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">rats from the other groups were sacrificed simultaneously.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Echocardiography</kw></strong><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

In four rats from each group, echocardiographical studies were</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">performed under pentobarbital anaesthesia before and 4 weeks</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">after the administration of monocrotaline using a 12&nbsp;MHz</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">phased array transducer (Sonos 5500; Hewlett-Packard Inc, Andover,</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">MA, USA). Ventricular dimensions and flow profiles over the</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">aortic, pulmonary and tricuspid valve were measured in standard</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">views </kw><a href="#R13"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">13</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. Cardiac output (mL·min</kw><sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">–1</kw></sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">) was calculated</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">using the following equation: </kw><a name="E001"><!-- null --></a>
<sup> </sup><br clear="right"></p><p><table border="0" width="100%"><tbody><tr><td align="center">
<sup> </sup><br clear="right"><p align="center"><img src="/content/vol26/issue3/images/medium/erj260487E001.gif" alt="{erj260487E001}" border="0" height="22" width="189"></p></td><td align="right" valign="bottom" width="20"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">(001)</kw></td></tr></tbody></table><sup> </sup></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Haemodynamics</kw></strong><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

At sacrifice, animals were anesthetised and ventilated with</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">room air. </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">P</kw></i><sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">AP</kw></sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">s were measured according to Rabinovitch </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">et al</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">.</kw><sup> </sup><a href="#R14"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">14</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. If these pressures could not be obtained, right ventricular</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">systolic pressure was recorded as being equal to systolic </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">P</kw></i><sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">AP</kw></sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Systemic arterial pressures were measured </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">via</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> the left carotid</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">artery.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Pathology</kw></strong><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

After haemodynamic measurements, the thorax was opened and the</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">presence of pleural fluid was noted. Atria, ventricles and lungs</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">were weighed separately. The left lung was fixed in 3.6% formalin.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Sections were stained with haematoxylin-eosin and resorcin-fuchsin</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">elastic stain </kw><a href="#R15"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">15</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> for morphometric analysis of vascular dimensions</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">using routine staining procedures. Additional sections were</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">stained with antibodies against endothelial nitric oxide synthase</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">(eNOS; dilution 1:500; BD Transduction Laboratories, Lexington,</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">KY, USA) and </kw><img src="/math/agr.gif" alt="{alpha}" border="0"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">-smooth muscle actin (Dako, Glostrup, Denmark).</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Paraffin sections were dewaxed and incubated with monoclonal</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">antibodies for 1&nbsp;h. Rabbit anti-mouse immunoglobulin G</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">coupled to peroxidase was used as a secondary antibody (dilution</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">1:100). The sections were stained with diaminobenzidine for</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">10&nbsp;min and counterstained with haematoxylin.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p>

DISCUSSION
</kw></font></th></tr></tbody></table>
<table align="right" border="1" cellpadding="5"><tbody><tr><th align="left"><font size="-1">
<a href="#top"><img alt=" " src="/icons/toc/uarrow.gif" border="0" height="9" hspace="5" width="11"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">TOP</kw><br></a>
<a href="#ABS"><img alt=" " src="/icons/toc/uarrow.gif" border="0" height="9" hspace="5" width="11"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">ABSTRACT</kw><br></a>
<a href="#SEC1"><img alt=" " src="/icons/toc/uarrow.gif" border="0" height="9" hspace="5" width="11"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">METHODS</kw><br></a>
<a href="#SEC2"><img alt=" " src="/icons/toc/uarrow.gif" border="0" height="9" hspace="5" width="11"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">RESULTS</kw><br></a>
<img alt=" " src="/icons/toc/dot.gif" border="0" height="9" hspace="5" width="11"><font color="#464c53"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">DISCUSSION</kw></font><br>
<a href="#BIBL"><img alt=" " src="/icons/toc/darrow.gif" border="0" height="9" hspace="5" width="11"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">REFERENCES</kw><br></a>
</font></th></tr></tbody></table>
&nbsp;<br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

The aim of the present study was to investigate the role of</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">increased pulmonary blood flow on pulmonary vascular remodelling</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">in pulmonary arterial hypertension. Therefore, an aortacaval</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">shunt was created in monocrotaline-treated rats. The major finding</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">was that compared with nonshunted monocrotaline-treated rats,</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">shunted monocrotaline rats showed increased morbidity and mortality.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">This was associated with increased pulmonary-to-systemic artery</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">pressure ratios, increased right ventricular hypertrophy and</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">dilatation, and a higher incidence of tricuspid valve regurgitation.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Pulmonary vascular histopathology and morphometry revealed no</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">additional effects of flow on the intra-acinar pulmonary vessel</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">remodelling. These findings suggest that the effects of an aortocaval</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">shunt on morbidity and mortality could be attributed to more</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">pronounced right heart failure, rather than to altered intra-acinar</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">pulmonary vessel remodelling.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Pre-acinar pulmonary arteries did increase in number and size</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">compared with both isolated increased pressure as well as isolated</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">increased flow. The present study, however, does not allow differentiation</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">between a secondary dilatatory effect of increased flow and</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">an intrinsic remodelling process of these arteries as a cause</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">for this observation.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Effects of increased pulmonary flow on pulmonary vascular remodelling</kw></strong><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

In the current study, rats with isolated increased pulmonary</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">blood flow did not develop changes in </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">P</kw></i><sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">AP</kw></sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> and showed a balanced</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">increase in weight of all cardiac compartments. Pulmonary vascular</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">morphometry after 5–6 weeks did not show significant changes</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">compared with control animals. This is congruent with previous</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">reports, where vascular changes usually occur only after much</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">longer periods of exposure to high flow conditions </kw><a href="#R8"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">8</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R16"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">16</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Monocrotaline-treated animals without shunt, displayed increased</kw><sup> </sup><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">P</kw></i><sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">AP</kw></sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">s and right ventricular hypertrophy, while morphometry revealed</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">increased wall thickness and increased muscularisation in the</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">pre-acinar pulmonary arteries, as expected from earlier studies</kw><sup> </sup><a href="#R3"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">3</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">–</kw><a href="#R5"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">5</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. Interestingly, occlusion of the intra-acinar vessels</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">was also found in monocrotaline-treated rats. These findings</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">are in contrast with previous studies reporting that intima</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">proliferation is caused by the addition of flow in the monocrotaline-treated</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">rats. In these studies, luminal occlusion was not reported in</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">the monocrotaline-treated rats without increased pulmonary flow</kw><sup> </sup><a href="#R9"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">9</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R11"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">11</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. Conversely, the present results are in accordance with</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Nishimura </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">et al</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. </kw><a href="#R10"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">10</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, who recently described luminal occlusion</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">in pulmonary arterioles in the monocrotaline rat model. In most</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of these studies </kw><a href="#R10"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">10</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R11"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">11</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R17"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">17</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R18"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">18</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> a visual scoring system was</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">used to assess the severity of neo-intimal formation in the</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">smaller pulmonary arteries or the examination was limited to</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">larger pulmonary arteries </kw><a href="#R9"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">9</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R19"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> 19</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">–</kw><a href="#R21"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">21</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, whereas the present</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">study provides qualitative and quantitative morphological characterisation</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of the pulmonary vasculature.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Animals that received both monocrotaline and a shunt displayed</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">an increased morbidity and mortality compared with all other</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">groups. In this respect, the current results contrast with the</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">conclusion of Nishimura </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">et al</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. </kw><a href="#R10"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">10</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, who suggested a salvage effect</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of additional flow in monocrotaline-treated rats using the same</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">model. However, in their experimental design, monocrotaline</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">was injected 1 week after the creation of the aortocaval shunt.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Studies in the authors' laboratory showed less pronounced disease</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">development when procedural order was used from the study by</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Nishimura </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">et al</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. </kw><a href="#R10"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">10</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (data not shown). In the current authors'</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">opinion, this can be explained by the fact that monocrotaline</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">is metabolised in the liver into toxic, but unstable, metabolites</kw><sup> </sup><a href="#R22"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">22</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. These toxic metabolites have a half-life of a few seconds</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">or less. Therefore, they have to exert their toxic effect in</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">the initial passage through the lungs. Increased flow by an</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">aortocaval shunt will lower the concentration of the metabolites</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">transported to the lung. This may explain why induction of a</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">shunt before the administration of monocrotaline will lead to</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">a diminished effect of monocrotaline and less advanced pulmonary</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">vascular disease.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

The nature and severity of the histological changes in the present</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">study are comparable with those reported in other combination</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">models of high pulmonary pressure and flow in the rat </kw><a href="#R6"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">6</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R9"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">9</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">,</kw><sup> </sup><a href="#R11"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">11</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. However, the origin of the luminal occlusion of intra-acinar</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">vessels, which is often referred to as neo-intima, is less clear.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">In the present study, the intraluminal occlusion consisted of</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">cellular material. Both endothelial cells and vascular smooth</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">muscle cells were present in this neo-intima, since it consisted</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of cells that stained positive for eNOS and of cells that stained</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">positive for </kw><img src="/math/agr.gif" alt="{alpha}" border="0"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">-smooth muscle actin.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Effects of increased pulmonary flow on the heart</kw></strong><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Although </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">P</kw></i><sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">AP</kw></sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> in shunted monocrotaline rats was comparable with</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">values in nonshunted monocrotaline rats, systemic blood pressure</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">was lower. The lower systemic blood pressures could be attributed</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">to a general decrease in cardiac output at the time of sacrifice.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The pronounced right ventricular hypertrophy and increased right</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">to left atrial weight ratios do suggest that right ventricular</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">failure, rather than biventricular high output failure, was</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">the major determinant of heart failure.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Echocardiography in rats was established as a useful diagnostic</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">tool for the cardiac assessment of abnormal pulmonary haemodynamics,</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">studying right ventricular wall thickness and ventricular dimensions.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The echocardiographical findings of progressive right ventricular</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">dilatation and the development of tricuspid regurgitation in</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">the shunted monocrotaline-treated animals support the diagnosis</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of right ventricular failure in these animals.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Progression of clinical deterioration and right ventricular</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">hypertrophy were correlated with increased luminal occlusion</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of the small pulmonary vessels, indicating that the development</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of right ventricular failure is aggravated by the detrimental</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">combination of increased volume load and increased afterload</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of the right ventricle.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Taken together, the results suggest that increased morbidity</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">and mortality in the shunted monocrotaline rats could be attributed</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">to effects on the heart, rather than to altered pulmonary vascular</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">remodelling.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Limitations of the study</kw></strong><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

In the present model, monocrotaline could be regarded as a confounding</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">factor to flow, because its metabolites induce direct pulmonary</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">endothelial cell damage that might possibly obscure the effects</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of flow on these endothelial cells.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

However, neo-intimal formation, a key feature in pulmonary vascular</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">remodelling in pulmonary arterial hypertension, is induced in</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">this model. Other models, creating left to right shunts in young</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">animals </kw><a href="#R16"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">16</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R23"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">23</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, </kw><a href="#R24"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">24</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> might have advantages with respect to affecting</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">flow and pressure in the pulmonary arteries comparable with</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">the situation in congenital heart disease. However, the increase</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">in </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">P</kw></i><sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">AP</kw></sub><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> in these models is moderate and, until now, they failed</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">to display the more advanced lesions of pulmonary arterial hypertension.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">These lesions distinguish pulmonary arterial hypertension from</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">other forms of pulmonary hypertension and, therefore, probably</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">form an important clue in the pathophysiology of pulmonary arterial</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">hypertension.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

Furthermore, the present study does not provide an answer to</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">the question as to whether increased pulmonary flow induced</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">specific vascular cell responses at a molecular level, which</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">might explain why in congenital heart defects the advanced pulmonary</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">vascular lesions of pulmonary arterial hypertension occur almost</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">exclusively in the presence of increased pulmonary blood flow.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p>

<strong><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Conclusion</kw></strong><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

The present study aimed to investigate the role of increased</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">flow in flow-associated pulmonary hypertension. Detrimental</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">effects of increased pulmonary blood flow in the aortacaval-shunted</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">monocrotaline rat model were demonstrated by increased morbidity</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">and mortality. These effects could, however, not be attributed</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">to histopathological aggravation of pulmonary vascular remodelling.</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">In contrast to earlier reports, the neo-intimal reaction described</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">in the shunted monocrotaline-treated rats in the present study</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">was not identified as a specific effect of the increased pulmonary</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">flow, as it was demonstrated to also be present in rats treated</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">with monocrotaline solely.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

In conclusion, haemodynamical, echocardiographical and pathological</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">data suggested pronounced right ventricular failure to be the</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">cause for the increased morbidity and mortality, rather than</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">altered pulmonary vascular remodelling. Further research on</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">the specific role of increased pulmonary blood flow in the development</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">of advanced pulmonary vascular lesions in pulmonary arterial</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">hypertension, associated with congenital heart defects, is needed</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">to understand the pathogenesis of this progressive pulmonary</kw><sup> </sup><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">vascular disease.</kw><sup> </sup></p><p>
