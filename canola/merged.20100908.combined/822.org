      <span class="country"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">International</kw></span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> — 
      The biggest names in electronics have just sat their first global exam on their green credentials. Ranked on their use of toxic chemicals and electronic waste (e-waste) policies only Dell and Nokia scraped a barely respectable score while Apple, Motorola and Lenovo flunked the test to finish bottom of the class.
    </kw></p>
    
    <div class="body"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">    
      









Our scorecard highlights which of the major electronics companies is
doing the most to remove the worst toxic chemicals from their products
and which companies have good recycling programs for their products</kw><br>
<br><h2><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Full ranking </kw></h2>
<a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/campaigns/toxics/electronics/how-the-companies-line-up"><img src="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/assets/graphics/green-electronics-guide"></a><br>
<br><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/campaigns/toxics/electronics/how-the-companies-line-up"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">Click here for the ranking</kw></a> <br>
<br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">
The ranking is important because the amounts of toxic e-waste is
growing everyday and it often ends up dumped in the developing world.
Reducing the toxic chemicals in products reduces pollution from old
products and makes recycling safer, easier and cheaper. Companies with
good recycling schemes help ensure that their products don't end up in
the e-waste yards of Asia.</kw><br>
<br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">
"The scorecard will provide a dynamic tool to green the electronics
sector by setting off a race to the top. By taking back their discarded
products, companies will have incentives to eliminate harmful
substances used in their products, since this is the only way they can
ensure safe reuse and recycling of electronic waste," said Iza Kruszewska, Greenpeace International toxics campaigner.</kw><br>
<br><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-nokia-rank"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">
Nokia</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> and </kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-dell-ranki"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Dell</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> share the top spot in the ranking. They believe that as
producers they should bear individual responsibility for taking back
and reusing or recycling their own-brand discarded products. Nokia
leads the way on eliminating toxic chemicals, since the end of 2005 all
new models of mobiles are free of polyvinyl chloride (PVC) and all new
components to be free of brominated flame retardants (BFRs) from the
start of 2007. Dell has also set ambitious targets for eliminating
these harmful substances from their products.</kw><br>
<br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">
Third place goes to </kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-hp-ranking"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">HP</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, followed by </kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-sony-erics"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Sony Ericsson</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (4th), </kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-samsung-ra"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Samsung</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (5th),
</kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-sony-ranki"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Sony</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (6th), </kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-lg-electro"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">LG Electronics</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (7th), </kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-panasonic"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Panasonic</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (8th), </kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-toshiba-ra"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Toshiba</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (9th),
</kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-fujitsu-si"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Fujitsu Siemens Computers</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (10th), </kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-apple-rank"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Apple</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (11th), </kw><a href="http://www.greenpeace.org/international/press/reports/greener-electronics-acer-ranki"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Acer</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> (12th) and
