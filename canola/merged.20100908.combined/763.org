    <span class="style9"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Sri Lanka Attack Kills Four Civilians, Two Sailors (Update1) </kw></span><span class="style5">    <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">      May 1 (Bloomberg) -- Two Sri Lankan sailors and four
civilians were killed in a mine explosion in the northeastern
port of Trincomalee, the latest incidence of violence threatening
a return to civil war in the South Asian island nation.          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> Police said the mine was attached to a bicycle left at the
side of a road and exploded as sailors inspected the bike during
a road-clearing operation, according to the TamilNet Web site.
The blast occurred today at 9:45 a.m. local time.          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> The dead were all Tamils and included a male driver and a
mother and her two children. Three sailors and two civilians were
wounded in the blast. Sri Lankan naval vessels earlier today
prevented an attack in Trincomalee port aimed at sailors going on
leave, the Defense Ministry said in a statement on its Web site.          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> An April 25 suicide bombing wounded the country's army chief,
prompting air strikes by the military on positions held by the
Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam in the north. The Tamil Tiger
rebels said the raids amounted to genocide. Violence has
increased since the government and the LTTE met in February to
strengthen a 2002 cease-fire, their first meeting in three years.          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> The Tamil Tigers have been fighting for two decades for a
separate homeland in the north and east of the country.          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> In the attack that was foiled in Trincomalee, a navy vessel
was entering the harbor to pick up the sailors going on leave
when rebel boats, including suicide craft, approached before a
navy patrol boat drove them away, the Defense Ministry said in
its statement.          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2"> International Aid          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> International donors, who have pledged $4.5 in aid to Sri
Lanka on condition progress is made toward a peace settlement,
have called on both sides to come to peace talks. The U.S.,
Japan, the European Union and Norway, the leaders of the group of
donor nations, met last week in Oslo to discuss the violence.          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> The Tamil Tigers said last week the air strikes showed the
government has ``openly declared war.'' The group has denied
carrying out the suicide attack that wounded Lieutenant General
Sarath Fonseka and killed nine people.          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> President Mahinda Rajapakse said last week the bombing
showed that the LTTE has ``broken all the efforts toward peace.''
The government's air raids were the first major assault on the
rebels since the February 2002 truce.          </kw></p>
       <p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> The rebels pulled out of Norwegian-brokered talks that were
to have opened in Geneva last week. The two sides agreed in
February to prevent intimidation, acts of violence, abductions or
killings. The government said it will ensure no armed groups
other than its security forces carry weapons, while the Tamil
Tigers pledged to stop attacks on the military and police.          </kw></p>
