<h1><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Hawaiian Islands and Hot Spots</kw></h1>


<div class="MsoNormal">

<hr align="left" size="2" width="100%">

</div>


<h2><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Outline</kw><span style="">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></h2>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left: 33pt; text-indent: -0.25in;"><!--[if !supportLists]--><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">1.</kw><span style="font: 7pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><!--[endif]--><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">Introduction </kw><span style="">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left: 33pt; text-indent: -0.25in;"><!--[if !supportLists]--><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">2.</kw><span style="font: 7pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><!--[endif]--><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">Hawaiian Island Formation</kw></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left: 33pt;"><span style="font-family: Symbol;"><span style=""><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">°</kw></span></span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1"> Plate Tectonic Theory</kw></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left: 33pt;"><span style="font-family: Symbol;"><span style=""><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">°</kw></span></span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1"> Missing Link</kw></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left: 33pt;"><span style="font-family: Symbol;"><span style=""><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">°</kw></span></span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1"> Answer</kw></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left: 33pt;"><span style="font-family: Symbol;"><span style=""><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">°</kw></span></span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">What Are Hot Spots?</kw></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left: 33pt;"><span style="font-family: Symbol;"><span style=""><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">°</kw></span></span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1"> Stationary Thermal Plumes</kw></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left: 33pt;"><span style="font-family: Symbol;"><span style=""><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">°</kw></span></span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1"> Effusive Eruption</kw></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">3.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">Conclusion</kw></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left: 0.5in;"><!--[if !supportEmptyParas]-->&nbsp;<!--[endif]--><o:p></o:p></p>


<div class="MsoNormal">

<hr align="left" size="2" width="100%">

</div>


<p><b><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Introduction</kw><o:p></o:p></b></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">When you think of islands, especially Hawaii, the first
thought that crosses one’s mind is warm weather, sunny beaches, and
coconuts.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">But have you ever wondered
how islands such as the Hawaiian chain islands were formed?</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">It is an amazing concept that a piece of
land is located out in the middle of a large ocean.</kw><b><o:p></o:p></b></p>

<p><b><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Hawaiian Island Formation</kw><o:p></o:p></b></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">When discussing the formation of islands, especially the
Hawaiian Islands, the theory of plate tectonics must be reviewed.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The earth is broken into 12 major plates 50-100
kilometers thick, which move horizontally.</kw><span style="">&nbsp;
</span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">These plates float and slide along the earth’s mantle, which explains
why most of the world’s volcanic eruptions and earthquakes occur here. </kw></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><!--[if !supportEmptyParas]-->&nbsp;<!--[endif]--><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">How does the theory of plate tectonics relate to formation
of the Hawaiian Islands? It doesn’t really, and that is the amazing concept
behind theses Hawaiian Islands.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">95
percent of the world’s known volcanoes occur close to these plate margins, but
what about the remaining 5 percent of volcanoes that aren’t occurring at the
plate boundary?</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Hawaiian volcanoes
occur well within the Pacific Plate, close to 4,000 kilometers from the nearest
plate boundary. How then could this chain of islands form in the middle of a
plate away from its boundaries if the center of volcanic activity occurs at
plate boundaries?</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><!--[if !supportEmptyParas]-->&nbsp;<!--[endif]--><o:p></o:p></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">This was a major puzzle in relation to plate tectonics.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">A Canadian scientist, J. Tuzo Wilson,
developed this answer.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Wilson, who was
one of the co-authors of the plate tectonic theory, suggested in the 1960’s
that the convection in the underlying mantle produced what we now know as hot
spots, and that the islands arose from magma poking through the sea floor as
the plate moved over the hot spot.</kw><span style="">&nbsp;
</span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Wilson looked to the Hawaiian Island Chain as dramatic proof of plate movement.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Stretching both to the west and north of
Hawaii, there is a string of smaller islands and submerged volcanoes about
3,700 miles long.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Each of these islands
or seamounts (a volcanic mountain found below sea level) is said to have formed
in the location of Hawaii presently.</kw><span style="">&nbsp;
</span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Adding to Wilson’s theory, Jason Morgan furthered the idea of the hot
spot.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Morgan suggested a large amount
of excess energy was welling up from deep within the planet.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Many scientists questioned Morgan’s theory,
stating the Hawaiian chain of islands has a bend in it.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Morgan claimed the reason for the bend was
because the hot spot is stationary, concluding that the Pacific Plate changed
direction around the age of the islands at the bend.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><b><!--[if !supportEmptyParas]-->&nbsp;<!--[endif]--><o:p></o:p></b></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">In simple terms, hot spots appear to be plumes of molten
material that well up from a fixed position deep within the earth as the plates
move above them.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">But it’s not that
simple.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Earthquakes play a major role
in the hot spot formation.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The location
of earthquakes at depth indicates the process through which molten rocks well
upward.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The force comes from lighter
molten rock surrounded by dense crystalline rocks.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">This, in return, changes the temperature and pressure, breaking
the hard surrounding rock, which causes earthquakes.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The magma comes from a layer 30-37 miles below the
plate.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The magma then rises to the
shallow chamber passing through the mantle and the crust and finally reaching
the surface.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">As the magma reaches the
surface, it cools quickly and forms pillow lava.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">This pillow lava rests on the contact edges of the volcano
growing quickly as magma.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">With time,
these developing volcanoes-sea mounts- become complex until finally breaking
the surface of the ocean and become an island.</kw><span style="">&nbsp;
</span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Wilson then explained the Hawaiian volcanic chain of islands.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">He proposed that there was an upwelling of
hot plastic rock originating deep beneath the plate.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">This plume rises by very slow convection and causes some melting
because of decompression as the hot rock rises into the mantle.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">This plume and its hot spot stay in one
location while the plate slides over this magma.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">A volcano then develops over the hot spots and then drifts away
with the moving plate.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">This process is
repeated continuously as long as the plates remain in motion and the hot spot
remains active.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><b><!--[if !supportEmptyParas]-->&nbsp;<!--[endif]--><o:p></o:p></b></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">So how do we explain the continuous thermal plume at a
constant spot?</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The heat source comes
from the mantle or even the core.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">This
indicates that the magma used in hot spots comes from a deeper source than
volcanoes at plate boundaries.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">In fact,
the Hawaiian hot spot has been active for 80 million years.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">If the theory of hot spots holds true, the
island furthest away from the hot spot should be the oldest and the island
closest to the hot spot should be the youngest.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Also, the aging of volcanoes away from the hot spot should match
the rate of plate movement. The Hawaiian Island chain indicates that the plate
moved 10 cm per year.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The hot spot
theory also explained why the Hawaiian Islands only have active volcanoes at the
southeast end of the chain.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The hot
spot explanation suggests that another island will soon form.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The next to-be island, which is in the
works, is the Loihi seamount.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><b><!--[if !supportEmptyParas]-->&nbsp;<!--[endif]--><o:p></o:p></b></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Most hot spot volcanoes are effusive.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">This is concept explains how the islands are
formed.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">An effusive eruption refers to
the type of eruption that characterizes Hawaii.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Effusive is a type of eruption in which the gas content in the
magma is low and the magma is less cohesive causing the gases to boil out less
violently.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span></p>

<p><b><kw class="krdwrd-tag-2">Conclusion</kw><o:p></o:p></b></p>

<p><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">New discoveries are made every day and it becomes more evident that the
study of volcanoes, plate tectonics and hot spots are all very closely related
to the formation of the Hawaiian Islands.</kw><span style="">&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">So the next time you think about Hawaii, don’t just consider the sun,
sand, and beaches.</kw><span style="">&nbsp; </span><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Think about this
amazing creation of island formation through the process of hot spots and
realize just how complex the world really is. </kw></p>
