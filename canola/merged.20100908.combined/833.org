					</tbody></table>	<font color="#000000" face="ARIAL, SANS SERIF" size="2"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Mocko Jumbies. Conch fritters and kallaloo. White sand beaches bordering azure blue water. Rum daiquiris, flips, sours, and fizzes. All this plus sunny days and warm temperatures moderated by sea breezes. Sounds just like Indiana – </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">not</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">! No wonder so many Midwesterners head to the U. S. Virgin Islands for a spring break!</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Let’s start with the Mocko Jumbies. Close your eyes and imagine really tall Caribbean clowns. Mocko Jumbies are colorfully costumed and masked stilt dancers that trace their origins to West African legends. With amazing agility and astonishing grace, they ward off bad spirits and welcome good ones, prancing about on their impossibly long legs.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> 


They turn up whenever there’s a Caribbean celebration, and the U.S. Virgin Islands is known for its parties -- planned and impromptu. In fact, if you’re feeling blue that you missed Mardi Gras celebrations in New Orleans, St. Thomas has a month-long carnival starting right after Easter. Mocko Jumbies are the tallest part of a day-long parade stepping off at 10 a.m., May 3, with music, floats, and lots of dancing. (I was there last year for the 50th anniversary of carnival -- it’s a non-stop party.)</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Then there’s the food. Island specialties include fritters made from conch (pronounced “konk”) -- the sea creature found inside those shells we’ve all held up to our ears to hear the sea. Kallaloo is traditional soup made of seafood and greens plus more -- maybe pork, okra, or garlic. Each cook makes it differently so you’ll never have the same bowl twice.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">

As for the rum, well, it’s an island institution. Locally made brands are Cruzan and Pusser. Experiment beyond piña coladas and strawberry daiquiris -- let the bartender make you his specialty, sit back and relax.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


There are 50 islands belonging to the U.S. (some no more than outcroppings of rock), but the big three are St. Thomas, St. John, and St. Croix. </kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Day-trippers -- the hordes that disembark from cruise ships -- head straight for the shops and miss most everything else.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


The shopping, of course, is legendary. That’s because there’s no sales or luxury tax, and no customs duties on items such as watches, cameras, liquor, jewelry, perfume, china, and leather goods. U.S. citizens are allowed a duty-free shopping quota of $1,200, twice that of any other island in the Caribbean and three times that of European countries. In their frenzy to go home with bargains from around the world, the cruise ship people miss the beauty of these particular islands.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


It’s much more fun to spend a week or so exploring. The islands have year-round subtropical weather with temperatures that fluctuate within a narrow range -- from an average of 77 degrees in the winter and up to 82 degrees in the summer. It rains often, but usually after dark. The lush vegetation is tropical, with flowers everywhere. Surrounding everything is the most beautiful water I’ve ever seen. Blue doesn’t begin to describe the liquid that shades from transparent to turquoise to sapphire to indigo as the depth increases.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Speaking of the water, my favorite spot on St. Thomas is Coral World. This isn’t your normal aquarium because the star attractions are still roaming free in the sea. It’s more of a Marine Park. You view the undersea world through submarine windows in an underwater observatory -- or don a diving helmet and see it up close and personal on a sea trek (an underwater hike). Of course, there are also fish tanks, a shark pool, and a “petting zoo” (starfish aren’t cuddly even when alive). But the chance to get nose-to-nose with a swimming fish isn’t something that happens every day.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Christopher Columbus landed on St. Croix in 1493, bringing a long period of European colonization to the islands. The U.S. purchased this portion of the Lesser Antilles from Denmark in 1917. St. Croix -- the largest -- retains more Danish flavor than the others do. Many large sugar plantations were located there, and its two major towns were laid out European-style grids.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Christiansted, the St. Croix capital, has a downtown with many Danish-built structures, including Fort Christiansvaern, which still dominates the waterfront. Built in 1749, it protected the port from pirates (including such legendary bad guys as Blackbeard).</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


These days the island is a favorite haunt of artists (more than 60 claim St. Croix as at least a part-time residence). A stroll through the galleries is a delight. During the winter season there’s an art walk from 5-9 p.m., one Thursday evening a month. (I bought a glass fish, handmade by Jan R. Mitchell.)</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Charlotte Amalie, the largest town on St. Thomas, also retains remnants of its European past. Towering over the typical traffic of a bustling commercial center with 48,000 residents and countless visitors is the tall, redbrick clock tower of Fort Christian (now a museum), dating from 1672.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


In contrast, the island of St. John is mostly wilderness. That’s because Laurence Rockefeller fell in love with the unspoiled beauty of the place in the 1950s. He bought and gifted 5,000 acres to the federal government for a national park (it takes up two-thirds of the island’s 19 square miles). Because there’s little room for development, this is the best island for quiet times on almost empty beaches.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


I also wandered one of that island’s most famous ruins. The Annaberg Historic Trail (part of the Virgin Islands National Park) leads through what was once the largest Dutch sugar plantation in the islands. At the highest point is the windmill, which crushed the cane grown on terraced hillsides. When it was built about 1780, 25 active sugar-producing factories were on the island, along with tobacco, cotton, and indigo fields.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


And, of course there’s an Indianapolis connection. I ran into Roger W. Morgan on St. Croix. He spent a decade or so as a WIFE disc jockey last century before moving to the islands. These days he produces Rock &amp; Roll Rewind, “the most listened-to oldies show in the world.”</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Next time I go, I’m staying longer -- and bringing a snorkel and mask. Off the shores of St. Croix is Buck Island Reef -- the only underwater national monument in the United States.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


THE DETAILS --</kw><br>

<a href="http://www.coralworldvi.com"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Coral World</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> Marine Park and Undersea Observatory, 6450 Coki Point, St. Thomas, USVI 00802, 1-888-695-2073. It’s open from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m., daily (including holidays). Admission is $18 for adults, plus an extra $68 if you want to go Sea Trekkin’. </kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Where to stay -- The choices range from rustic cabins to the most posh resorts you can imagine. One of the more interesting choices on St. Croix is </kw><a href="http://www.hotelcaravelle.com"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Hotel Caravelle</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. Instead of being on a remote beach, it’s right in the middle of Christiansted, just steps from the shops and galleries.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Where to eat -- I sampled a dozen restaurants in all sorts of price ranges, and didn’t have a bad meal during my week-long stay on the islands. My favorites on St. Thomas are </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The Inn at Blackbeard’s Castle</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> and </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">The Old Stone Farm House</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. On St. Croix, I especially enjoyed </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Indies</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3"> and </kw><i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">Kendrick’s</kw></i><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. </kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


The U.S. Virgin Islands is about 1,500 miles southeast of New York City. The islands were formed about 100 million years ago, from the undersea crust that rose to the surface during volcanic activity. There haven’t been any eruptions lately, but watch out for hurricane season! </kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


All persons born on the islands are citizens of the United States and are governed by U.S. law. The U.S. Virgin Islands is an unincorporated territory, and sends a non-voting delegate to the U.S. House of Representatives. The currency is the U.S. dollar, ATMs are everywhere, credit cards are widely accepted, and postage is the same as stateside.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


I thought I might rent a car on St. Thomas, but changed my mind when I discovered that the traffic pattern is English (yes, they drive on the left). Cabs and multi-passenger transit vans carried me around the islands just fine. </kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


Getting from island to island is easy. St. John is only 20 minutes away from St. Thomas by </kw><a href="http://www.virginislandsfastferry.com"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">ferry</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">. Regularly scheduled small-plane service, </kw><a href="http://www.seaborneairlines.com"><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">seaplanes</kw></a><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">, hydrofoil, and commercial airlines link St. Thomas and St. Croix. The inter-island flight takes about 20 minutes. Helicopter service also connects the islands.</kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


The U.S. Virgin Islands is in the Atlantic Standard Time zone, one hour ahead of Indiana (it doesn’t observe Daylight Savings Time either). </kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-3">


U.S. citizens are not required to have passports when traveling to the U.S. Virgin Islands, but it sure makes life easier at departure when you will go through customs and immigration as though you’d been all the way outside the U.S. Without a passport you’ll need a government-issued photo ID and a certified copy of your birth certificate to prove citizenship. And, no, you can’t bring agricultural products (such as bananas or flowers) back to the states with you. </kw><br><br><kw class="krdwrd-tag-1">
