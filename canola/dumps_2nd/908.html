Latest tsunami information, resources
By Randy Lilleston, USATODAY.com
Did wild animals sense danger last month before the south Asia tsunami hit?

That's been an ongoing question, amid scattered reports of animals seeking safety just before the disaster. Although highly debatable, there's at least some validity in the theory that animals can sense some oncoming dangers, according to this National Geographic article, among others. (And since it's National Geographic, the article also includes a great photo of an elephant's eye.) Posted at 10:25 a.m. ET.

Travel: With so much of south Asia dependent on tourist dollars, the effect of the tsunami on travel is emerging as major news. Here are two USA TODAY articles on tourism developments: 1 | 2Posted at 11:08 a.m. ET.

U.N. information: The United Nations News Centre's Web site has created an area focused on tsunami developments, including the U.N.'s attempt to coordinate the emergency response. Posted at 10:20 a.m. ET.

USA TODAY's coverage: The latest USA TODAY tsunami coverage includes Kofi Annan's plea for $1 billion in quick cash; a poll indicating that nearly half of American households have given money to aid tsunami victims; and a look at the hardships being suffered in a Somali village. Posted at 10:17 a.m. ET.

Thursday, Jan. 6:

Kofi Annan's message to world leaders Thursday was simple: Cash, now.

"The disaster was so brutal, so quick, and so far-reaching, that we are still struggling to comprehend it," the U.N. secretary general told a tsunami disaster summit in Indonesia. He said pledges for aid need "to be converted into cash quickly ... It is a race against time." Here's audio of Annan at the conference. Posted at 9:09 a.m. ET.

Warning systems: One of the biggest ongoing debates in the tragedy involves a tsunami warning system — or in this case, a lack thereof. This Australian government site discusses warning systems and how they work. Posted at 1:52 p.m. ET.

Health issues: Another government agency keeping an eye on developments: the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention has a page dedicated to health issues in the stricken area. Posted at 11:42 a.m. ET.

More resources: The U.S. Agency for International Development has compiled this list of tsunami relief resources, including links to a number of charitable groups already operating in the region. Posted at 11:38 a.m. ET.

U.N. vs. aid groups: An emerging story from the disaster relief efforts involves the United Nations and private aid groups. The U.N. has fought corruption charges for years when it comes to aid programs, and a recent scandal involving Annan's son and the Iraq oil-for-food program hasn't helped. Some aid groups wonder why they weren't invited to Thursday's tsunami relief summit. Posted at 9:21 a.m. ET.

Wednesday, Jan. 5

For obvious reasons, much of the attention in the south Asia disaster so far has been focused on devastation caused by the tsunami, with less on the earthquake that triggered the ocean waves.

But the "megathrust earthquake" is itself a highly unusual natural phenomenon, and the results are devastating. Wikipedia takes a look at megathrust earthquakes, and notes that all five earthquakes of 9.0 or higher since 1900 were of this variety. Posted at 9:52 a.m. ET.

American deaths rise: The State Department raised the number of Americans presumed dead to 36 Wednesday, with thousands still missing. Posted at 12:25 p.m. ET.

Tsunami vs. war: It was inevitable that someone would take out a calculator and compare the $350 million U.S. government pledge against the continuing costs of the war in Iraq. The Pseudorandom blog calculates that $350 million would fund about 42 hours of the Iraq war, and provides a number of links to other blogs discussing the topic. Posted at 12:01 p.m. ET.

Unforeseen consequences: Among the dangers now faced by some tsunami survivors: Land mines in Sri Lanka, according to this article published last week in The Australian. Posted at 11:55 a.m. ET.

More tsunami science: Here's a web site for a PBS documentary on tsunamis. The site includes photos, videos and Flash animations. And the National Geophysical Data Center of the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration maintains a tsunami database. Posted at 9:57 a.m. ET.

Tuesday, Jan. 4:

There is a sad, predictable problem that arises amid the fundraising efforts that follow tragic events: Scam artists see an opportunity to make a quick buck.

The massive outpouring of cash since the Asian tsunami disaster offers opportunity for such scam artists — and even if you're not a victim of a scam, you probably still want to give your money to a charity that knows how to use it wisely. Here's a column that will help you do just that. Posted at 8:25 a.m. ET.

More on crime: Reuters is reporting on a worldwide crime wave aimed not just at potential donors, but at survivors and families of victims. Posted at 1:13 p.m. ET.

Finding the missing: A number of Web sites now are dedicated to assisting in locating missing persons in the area affected by the tsunami. You can find private sites here, here, here and here, among other places. And Thailand has set up an official site.Posted at 9:12 a.m. ET; updated at 1:15 p.m. ET.

More satellite images: DigitalGlobe, a private satellite imagery company, has an entire gallery of tsunami photos posted on its site. The before-and-afters, particularly from Banda Aceh, clearly illustrate the scope of the tsunami's devastation. Posted at 12:24 p.m. ET.

Secondary consequences: There has been a great deal of coverage about epidemics that often sweep through areas after a disaster strikes, but this tsunami will have other lasting consequences as well. For example, the Doctors Without Borders Web site notes that survivors are rapidly slaughtering livestock— not only raising concerns about sustaining herds in the future, but also causing intestinal problems triggered by being unable to properly cook meat. Posted at 11 a.m. ET.

The X-Presidents: If you missed it yesterday: Former presidents Bush and Clinton will head a massive U.S. private fundraising effort. Posted at 9:28 a.m. ET.

Video blogs: The tsunami disaster might mark the tipping point for video blogs — the natural progression of the blog world as home Web access gets faster and faster. There was an article Monday on the issue in The Wall Street Journal, featuring the Cheese and Crackers blog, which has been compiling a number of Web links to video. You can find other video blogs and/or links here, here and here, among other places — or just perform a Google search. And Wikipedia has a great page of tsunami information, including video links. Posted at 8:45 a.m. ET.

Monday, Jan. 3:

It might be easy to think of the Asian tsunami disaster as a regional issue, but the victims are from all over the world — including the United States.

Fifteen Americans have been reported dead in the region. There are reports that thousands remain unaccounted for, although estimates vary widely and the larger figures are being greeted with skepticism. The State Department has set up a toll-free number to inquire about the welfare of Americans in the area: 1-888-407-4747. State Department officials are asking Americans in the region to touch base with their families in the U.S. and with the nearest U.S. diplomatic post. Another potential source of information: The Tourism Authority of Thailand. Posted at 11:33 a.m.

Local news: Some of the most insightful post-disaster coverage is coming, as would be expected, from the local news media in the affected area. Asia is filled with English-language daily newspapers and news services, and among those offering tsunami coverage are The Malaysia Star, The Jakarta Post, The Times of India, The New Straits Times of Malaysia, The Straits Times of Singapore, The Daily Mirror of Sri Lanka and TamilEelam News Services, which backs Sri Lanka's long-standing Tamil separatist group. Posted at 2:01 p.m. ET.

From the sky: Here are some satellite images of coastlines hit by the tsunami, courtesy of the Centre for Remote Imaging, Sensing and Processing of the National University of Singapore. Posted at 1:41 p.m. ET.

Giving money: There are many nonprofit organizations that are taking donations to provide tsunami disaster relief — so many, in fact, that picking one (or a few) can be a confusing task. The Charity Navigator Web site can help you evaluate potential charities, and the site now has an article on how you can make an intelligent choice. Posted at 12:52 p.m. ET.

The politics of disaster: There's a disturbing report in the Sydney (Australia) Morning Herald that Indonesia's military is using the disaster as an excuse to accelerate a campaign against separatists in the Aceh region. An emergency summit is being planned in Jakarta for later this week; in one sign of the political instability in the region, a Turkish publication says 14,000 police will be dispatched to the summit to provide security. Posted at 12:24 p.m. ET.

More politics: Sri Lanka, where more than 28,000 have died from the tsunami, has political woes of its own. It's a land of divisions over religion and ethnicity, and Tamil separatists control the northern portion of the country. As aid rolls in, there are reports of squabbles over credit. U.S. Marines and sailors are arriving in the region and are expected to offer aid, but that may be a delicate political task in a divided nation. Posted at 12:34 p.m. ET.

First-hand accounts: Among the local blogs to spring up after the tsunami: 2BNewsfeed, which focuses on Thailand; the Southeast Asia Earthquake and Tsunami Blog; Tsunami Blog India, which features e-mailed requests (and offers) for help; and a number of purported first-person accounts on LiveJournal. Posted at 11:58 a.m. ET.

The aftermath: One of the horrible truths of a disaster of this size is that many people in the area who are healthy today may die in the next few weeks from disease outbreaks. UNICEF takes a look at the grim reality of trying to keep an estimated 1 million displaced people healthy. Among other efforts, the agency is distributing rehydration salts in India to treat diarrhea outbreaks that are attributed to contaminated water. Posted at 11:44 a.m. ET.
