 Stats Geek reinforces the notion that 'crime' doesn't pay

Tuesday, September 23, 2003

By Brian O'Neill

When you look at the way the Pirates have scored runs in the second half of the season, you see how unimportant the stolen base is in the 21st-century game.

Baseball has been built on a mountain of statistics, but only with the arrival of the computer age has the stats geek thrived. This is an occasional series looking at the Pirates through the eyes of a numbers junkie.

Before the All-Star break, the Pirates stole 49 bases and were caught 17 times. They were 14th in the league in runs scored.

Since the break, they've stolen only 32 bases and have been cut down 17 times. But they're second in the league in runs in the second half. Even as their stolen base success rate dropped from 74 percent to 65 percent, the Pirates run production jumped from 4.45 runs per game to 5.15. More important, their 32-33 record beats their 41-50 mark before the break.

Means nothing, right? That's just one team. But I'd challenge anyone to name a National League club that benefits greatly from steals. This game is all about getting on base and hitting the long ball now.

The top eight teams in runs scored are the Braves, Cardinals, Rockies, Astros, Phillies, Pirates, Marlins and Giants. The same eight teams top the league in on base percentage plus slugging percentage (OPS) in nearly the same order: Braves, Cardinals, Rockies, Phillies, Astros, Pirates, Giants and Marlins.

The link between runs and OPS roughly continues through the bottom of the league, with the Dodgers dead last in both categories. The Dodgers are tied for fifth in stolen bases.

The top four teams in stolen bases through Saturday were Florida (145), Montreal (99), Milwaukee (96) and the Pirates (81). These top four stealing teams are seventh, ninth, 11th and sixth in runs scored.

Ah, but there are times when your team could use that extra base to help get that extra run to win the game, right? True. But what about the runners you lose and the outs you give away when you fail?

Nobody can be sure where the tipping point is. An old rule of thumb has it that anything below a 70 percent success rate hurts your team, and 80 percent or better helps. But between those marks, teams run through murky ground.

Atlanta, 11th in the league in steals, has the best success rate at 76 percent. Eight other teams are 70 percent or better, including the Pirates (70). The Giants are worst at stealing, successful only 57 percent of the time. That could explain why they're seventh in OPS but only eighth in runs.

I could numb you with more teams' numbers, but let's close with a glance at Kenny Lofton.

I was among those who didn't think the Pirates should sign Lofton. I wanted an outfield of Reggie Sanders in left, Brian Giles in center and a platoon of Matt Stairs and Craig Wilson in right. I'd trade defense for more runs.

Then Lofton arrived and I ate crow. He hit .280 with 18 stolen bases in 23 tries (72 percent). He also hit nine home runs while playing a solid center field. Stairs, meantime, got off to a horrendous start before getting hurt. Craig Wilson rarely played.

So why did the Pirates score more runs after Lofton was traded to Chicago? Give some credit to his successor, Tike Redman, who has a .355 on-base percentage and .478 slugging percentage for an OPS of .833. Lofton's numbers as a Pirate were .333/.437/.770.

Stairs and Wilson also have been pounding the ball. With about a full season between them (581 ABs), these two have combined for 37 homers and 103 RBIs. They've had 23 homers, 64 RBIs and a .304 average since the break. Does anyone care that they've stolen only three bases all season? Does anyone want them to try again?

Rickey Henderson stole 130 bases in 1982. Only two teams are likely to steal more this season. One is Tampa Bay, on the way to 100 losses. The other is Florida, which has a chance at the playoffs. Florida can hit and pitch.
